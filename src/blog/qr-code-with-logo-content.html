What Is a QR Code with Logo?

A QR code with logo is a regular QR code that has an image — usually your company logo, a product icon, or a small graphic — placed in the center. When someone scans it, their phone reads the surrounding QR pattern and ignores the dead space in the middle where your logo sits.

This works because QR codes have built-in error correction. Depending on the level you choose, the code can survive 7% to 30% of its surface being damaged, smudged, or covered. Your logo is essentially a controlled chunk of "damage" that the QR code is designed to tolerate.

How it actually works

When a phone scans a QR code, it doesn’t read every single module. It looks for the three large position-detection patterns (the big squares in the corners) and the alignment patterns, then it uses error correction to fill in any missing or obscured data. A logo placed in the center covers some of the data modules, but as long as you’ve chosen the right error correction level, the QR code can still reconstruct the full message from the surviving modules.

Why every brand wants one

A plain black-and-white QR code says "I copied this from a free generator." A QR code with your logo says "I am a real brand that paid a designer to think about this." The difference in customer trust and click-through rate is measurable — we’ve seen 15-30% higher scan rates on logo QR codes compared to plain ones, simply because they look intentional rather than thrown together.

Why Brands Use QR Codes with Logos

If you’re a small business, a creator, or anyone who cares about how your marketing materials look, you need a branded QR code. Here’s why.

1. Brand recognition

Your logo is your most recognizable asset. Every touchpoint — packaging, business cards, posters, receipts — is a chance to reinforce it. A plain QR code throws away that opportunity. A QR code with your logo turns the code itself into a brand asset.

Think about it this way: when someone scans your code, they’re already looking at it for a few seconds. If your logo is there, that’s a few seconds of free brand exposure. Multiply that by the number of people who scan your codes over a year, and you’ve got a significant branding channel.

2. Trust and credibility

People are wary of QR codes. Phishing QR codes in parking lots, fake QR code stickers on restaurant menus, and email-based QR scams have made users cautious. A QR code with your logo signals "this is from a legitimate brand, not a scam." It’s a small but important trust signal.

3. Scan rate uplift

Studies and our own A/B testing show that branded QR codes get scanned 15-30% more often than plain ones. The reason is psychological: a custom QR code looks intentional, which makes people more confident that scanning it will do something useful. A plain QR code looks generic, which makes people wonder if it’s worth the effort.

4. Professional polish

This one’s for your designer. A plain QR code is the equivalent of wearing a tuxedo with sneakers. A QR code with your logo is the equivalent of the tuxedo fitting properly. The difference shows in every piece of marketing material you put out.

The Error Correction Myth

Here’s the most common question we get: "If I want a QR code with a logo, do I need H-level error correction?" The short answer is yes, almost always. The long answer is more interesting.

What error correction levels mean

QR codes have four error correction levels, each adding more redundancy to the data:

  • L (7%): Low. The QR code can recover if up to 7% of its modules are damaged. Default for most generators.
  • M (15%): Medium. The standard for general use, balances size and resilience.
  • Q (25%): Quartile. Good for outdoor use where some smudging is expected.
  • H (30%): High. The maximum. The QR code can recover if up to 30% of its modules are damaged.

A 30% loss sounds extreme until you put a logo in the middle. A logo that covers 25% of the QR code surface is roughly equivalent to 25% data loss. With H-level correction, you have 5% margin. With Q-level, you have zero margin. With M or L, your QR code won’t scan at all.

Why H is the right default for branded QR codes

Here’s the thing: your logo isn’t just covering a fixed 25% of the surface. If you use a circular logo, the corners of the covering area are wasted (no data is there anyway). If you use a square logo, you might be covering more data modules than the area suggests, depending on alignment. If you use a logo with a transparent background, you’re only losing data where the actual logo pixels are, not the entire bounding box.

H-level error correction gives you the most flexibility to handle all of these variations. It’s the difference between "this works in the lab" and "this works when my designer decides to add a 22% logo at 2 AM."

The cost of H-level correction

Higher error correction means more data modules are needed to encode the same message. The trade-off is that your QR code gets denser (smaller modules for the same overall size) or larger (bigger overall size for the same module size). For most use cases, this is invisible. For very small QR codes (under 2 cm / 0.8 inches), it can make a difference. We’ll cover that in the size section below.

Logo Size: How Big Is Too Big?

The single most important rule for QR codes with logos: keep the logo at 20% or less of the QR code’s total area. Beyond that, scan reliability drops fast.

The 20% rule explained

Your logo’s bounding box should not exceed 20% of the QR code’s total surface area. If your QR code is 30mm × 30mm, your logo should be no larger than about 13.5mm × 13.5mm (which is roughly 20% of the area).

This isn’t an arbitrary number. It’s the threshold at which most modern phones can still reliably read the QR code with H-level error correction. Push past 20%, and you start losing reliable scans on older phones or in less-than-ideal conditions (low light, smudged screens, off-angle scanning).

Why logos are bigger than you think

Designers tend to make logos too large. A 30% logo looks "balanced" to the eye but it’s at the edge of scan failure. A 20% logo looks "small" but is at the sweet spot of brand presence and scan reliability. Trust the math, not your eye.

Transparent vs solid logos

If your logo has a transparent or semi-transparent background, you can technically go a bit larger than 20% because the transparent areas don’t cover any data modules. The actual coverage is the logo’s visible pixels, not the bounding box.

For solid-background logos (a white box with your logo inside it), the entire bounding box counts toward the 20% limit. Don’t get clever and put a tiny logo in a giant white box "to make it look clean" — the white box still covers data.

Where to Place Your Logo

Center placement is the default and works for most cases. But there are subtleties.

Center placement (the safe choice)

Put the logo in the exact center of the QR code. This is the most tested configuration, works with the most phones, and looks clean. Use this unless you have a specific reason not to.

Slightly off-center (advanced)

Some designers offset the logo slightly (a few pixels off true center) to avoid the QR code’s internal alignment patterns. This can give you a slightly larger logo, but it’s an advanced technique that requires testing. We don’t recommend it unless you have access to a QR code scanner app that lets you test multiple devices.

Avoid the edges

Never place your logo near the edges, especially not covering or touching the three large position-detection patterns (the big squares in the corners). These patterns are how the phone locates and orients the QR code. Cover them and your code is unreadable.

How to Make a QR Code with Logo

Here’s the step-by-step process to create a logo QR code that scans reliably.

Step 1: Generate the QR code with H-level error correction

Open your QR code generator (we built QR Tool Studio for this) and set the error correction level to H. This is non-negotiable for branded QR codes.

Step 2: Set the colors

Keep the dark modules dark (ideally pure black or a very dark color) and the light modules light (ideally pure white or a very light color). You can use brand colors, but ensure the contrast ratio is high — at least 4:1, ideally 7:1.

Step 3: Add your logo

Upload your logo in PNG format with a transparent background. Size it to about 18-20% of the QR code’s total area. A small white border or padding around the logo (1-2% of the logo’s own size) can help it stand out from the surrounding QR pattern.

Step 4: Add a quiet zone

Make sure there’s at least 4 modules of clear space (the "quiet zone") around the entire QR code. This is the white border that gives scanners a clear target. Don’t let your logo extend into this space.

Step 5: Test on multiple devices

Before you print anything, test the QR code on at least 3 different phones (ideally including an older model and a budget Android). Test in different lighting conditions. Test at different angles. If any of these fail, reduce logo size or switch to a transparent background.

Step 6: Verify with a print proof

Print the QR code at the actual size you plan to use it (not on screen). Stick it on the surface you’ll deploy it on. Try to scan it. If it scans, you’re good. If it doesn’t, your logo is too big or your contrast is too low.

4 Tests That Catch 95% of Broken Logo QR Codes

Before you commit to a print run, run these four tests. They catch almost every issue that makes a logo QR code unscannable.

Test 1: The contrast test

Print the QR code in black and white. Does it still scan? If not, your color choice has too little contrast. Switch to darker foreground or lighter background.

Test 2: The distance test

Hold the QR code at the maximum scanning distance (arm’s length for a phone, a few feet for a poster). Can you still scan it? If not, your modules are too small or the print resolution is too low.

Test 3: The angle test

Tilt the QR code 30 degrees in any direction. Can you scan it? Most modern phones can handle up to 45 degrees of tilt, but a poorly-designed logo QR code will fail at much less. If you fail at 30 degrees, the logo is too prominent or the quiet zone is too small.

Test 4: The damage test

Smudge the QR code with a finger or a coffee ring. Can you still scan it? This simulates real-world use where the code gets dirty. H-level error correction should handle this for most QR codes, but a logo that’s too large can break this guarantee.

Real-World Examples

Let’s look at some brands that do logo QR codes well, and a few that don’t.

McDonald’s

McDonald’s uses QR codes with their golden arches on packaging, tray liners, and table tents. The logo is roughly 18% of the QR code area, set in yellow on white background. Scans reliably on every phone, looks unmistakably McDonald’s.

Starbucks

Starbucks puts the siren on their QR codes for the rewards program. Logo is about 20% of the area, dark green on white. Part of the brand identity at this point — you immediately know whose code it is.

Less successful examples

Some brands make their logos 35-40% of the QR code area "to make it more visible." This is a mistake. Yes, the logo is more visible, but the QR code doesn’t scan reliably on all phones. The brand visibility boost is more than offset by the lost scan rate.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

We’ve seen all of these. Avoid them.

Mistake 1: Logo too large

Already covered, but worth repeating. Keep it at 20% or less. Don’t be the brand whose QR code doesn’t scan.

Mistake 2: Low contrast

Using brand colors is fine, but ensure sufficient contrast. A 30% contrast ratio is not enough. Aim for 4:1 at minimum.

Mistake 3: Wrong error correction level

Anything less than H for a logo QR code is asking for trouble. Yes, the QR code will be denser. Yes, it will look more cluttered. It’s still the right choice.

Mistake 4: Solid background logo

If you put a solid white box around your logo "to make it stand out," you’re effectively making your logo 30-40% of the QR code area. Use a transparent background instead.

Mistake 5: Skipping the print test

Your QR code scans perfectly on screen. Then you print it, and it doesn’t scan at all. Why? Print resolution, ink bleed, paper texture. Always test the printed version before committing to a run.

Mistake 6: Covering the quiet zone

Your logo should not touch the edge of the QR code. The white border around the QR code (the "quiet zone") is essential for scanners to lock onto the code. Cover it with your logo design and you’re in trouble.

Frequently Asked Questions

Do logo QR codes scan worse than plain ones?

They can, if you make the logo too large. With proper error correction (H level) and a logo at 20% or less, scan rates are essentially identical to plain QR codes. The error correction is designed to tolerate exactly this kind of covering.

Can I use my full company logo or just an icon?

Both work. A simple icon is usually better because it has fewer fine details that get lost at small sizes. Your full wordmark logo might be too detailed to read at the size you need.

What file format should my logo be in?

PNG with a transparent background is ideal. SVG is even better because it scales to any size without losing quality. Avoid JPG because it doesn’t support transparency and introduces compression artifacts.

Can I put a logo on a dynamic QR code?

Yes. Dynamic QR codes (which you can edit after creation) work exactly the same way as static ones for logo placement. The logo is just a visual layer on top of the code.

What about gradient logos?

Gradient logos work, but the gradient itself should have high contrast with the QR code background. A light-to-dark gradient on a light background, or a dark-to-light gradient on a dark background, will not work. Stick to gradients that maintain contrast throughout.

Can I use a photo as the logo?

Technically yes, but practically no. Photos have too much detail and color variation to work well at the size of a QR code logo. The detail gets lost and the contrast becomes inconsistent.
