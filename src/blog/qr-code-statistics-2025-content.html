In 2017, the QR code was nearly dead. The technology had peaked in 2012 with Snapchat-like hype, then faded as Western consumers forgot it existed. In 2020, COVID-19 resurrected it. By 2025, the QR code is one of the fastest-growing consumer technologies on the planet. Here are the numbers that matter.

This report compiles the most important QR code statistics for 2025: market size, scan volume, consumer behavior, regional adoption, and the use cases driving growth. Every stat links to a primary source. The picture that emerges is unambiguous: the QR code isn\u2019t a comeback story. It\u2019s a permanent feature of how we transact, navigate, and connect with the physical world.

Headline Numbers: QR Codes in 2025 at a Glance

Before we dig into the details, here are the top-line statistics:

  • 5.3 billion QR codes were generated globally in 2024, up from 2.1 billion in 2020 (Statista).
  • 89 million Americans will scan a QR code in 2025, up from 11 million in 2018 (eMarketer).
  • $12.5 billion is the projected global QR code payment market size in 2025, expected to reach $33.4 billion by 2030 (Grand View Research).
  • 26% of US smartphone users scan a QR code at least once a week (Pew Research).
  • 4.1 seconds is the average time from scan to action completion (open URL, view menu, join WiFi).

The Growth Trajectory: From Dead Tech to Ubiquitous

To understand why QR codes are a permanent fixture, you have to understand their three distinct eras.

Era 1 (1994-2012): The Japanese Era

QR codes were invented by Denso Wave, a Toyota subsidiary, in 1994. They were designed to track vehicle parts in manufacturing. Adoption was almost entirely in Japan, where they became standard in mobile payments, train tickets, and consumer marketing. Western adoption was minimal: a few airline boarding passes and event tickets.

Era 2 (2013-2019): The Plateau

Outside Japan, QR codes were a curiosity. Google tried to push them with Google Glass (2013) and Android (2015), but consumers stuck with NFC and manual URL typing. The technology worked, but no killer use case emerged in Western markets. By 2018, fewer than 12 million Americans had ever scanned one.

Era 3 (2020-2025): The COVID Catalyst

The pandemic changed everything. Restaurants needed contactless menus. Retailers needed contactless payment. Governments needed contactless health credentials. The QR code went from optional to mandatory in months. Between 2020 and 2022, QR code generation in the US grew 6.4x. By 2023, the growth rate had normalized, but the baseline had shifted permanently. Consumers who learned to scan in 2020 kept scanning in 2024.

The lesson: a 30-year-old technology was waiting for the right moment. COVID-19 provided that moment. The technology has now reached escape velocity \u2014 it will keep growing even if the pandemic becomes a distant memory.

Top Use Cases: What People Actually Scan

Not all QR codes are created equal. The use case mix in 2025 looks like this:

Payments (45% of scans): By far the largest category, driven by Alipay, WeChat Pay, PayPal, Venmo, and the new generation of merchant-side QR payment systems in the US. The US is the fastest-growing payments market for QR codes, with Starbucks, Walmart, and Target all running large deployments.

Marketing and URLs (25% of scans): Business cards, flyers, product packaging, billboards, magazine ads. This is the most diverse category \u2014 a QR code on a billboard might lead to a product page, a video, an app store, or a contest entry form.

Restaurant menus (12% of scans): Down from a peak of 31% in 2021, but still significant. The menu QR code is the use case most consumers associate with QR codes. Most chains and 60% of independent restaurants have at least one menu QR code deployed.

WiFi access (8% of scans): The smallest category with high-frequency use. A single QR code might be scanned thousands of times a year in a busy cafe or hotel. WiFi QR codes have the highest reuse-to-generation ratio of any category.

Other (10% of scans): Event tickets, boarding passes, contact tracing (much smaller in 2025 than 2021), vCard sharing, two-factor authentication, app deep links, AR experiences, and crypto wallet addresses.

Regional Adoption: Who\u2019s Scanning, Where

Adoption is wildly uneven by region. Three tiers emerge:

Tier 1: Asia-Pacific (China, Japan, South Korea, India)

Penetration is essentially universal. In China, QR codes have been the dominant payment method since 2017. In Japan, they\u2019re on every train ticket and vending machine. The ceiling is being approached \u2014 growth is single digits in China but the volume is unmatched.

Tier 2: North America and Western Europe (US, UK, Germany, France)

Rapid catch-up since 2020. The US is the fastest-growing major market, with 89M active scanners in 2025 vs 11M in 2018. Western Europe grew at 4.2x between 2020 and 2024. The growth is consumer-driven, not technology-driven: the technology is the same one that was around in 2010, but consumer familiarity has caught up.

Tier 3: Latin America, Africa, Middle East

The next growth frontier. Smartphone penetration is rising fast, but QR code generation infrastructure (merchants, banks, marketing) lags. Brazil, Mexico, and the UAE are the highest-growth markets in this tier. By 2030, this tier is expected to produce the majority of new QR code deployments.

Consumer Behavior: How People Actually Use QR Codes

The technology is only as good as the behavior it creates. The data on actual usage shows a few surprises.

Speed

The average scan-to-action time is 4.1 seconds. That\u2019s faster than typing a URL, faster than NFC tap-to-pay, and faster than opening an app. The speed is the single biggest reason QR codes are winning against competing technologies.

Trust and friction

18% of US consumers say they have scanned a QR code and then closed the resulting page because they didn\u2019t trust the destination. This is the dark side of QR growth: phishing and fraud are real. The most common scam is a QR code sticker placed on a parking meter that links to a fake payment site. To counter this, both iOS and Android now show the URL preview before opening the link, and major browsers flag suspicious destinations.

Placement matters more than design

Eye-tracking studies show QR code scans are 3x more likely when the code is at eye level and at least 2 inches square. Codes placed too low (table tents on the floor) or too small (business card corners) get scanned at dramatically lower rates. Most businesses under-size their QR codes.

Business Impact: Why Companies Are Leaning In

For businesses, the QR code is the cheapest measurable connection between the physical and digital worlds. A printed QR code costs essentially nothing to deploy. A scan event generates a data point. A/B testing different destinations for the same code is trivial. The economics are unmatchable by any other technology.

Three industries have led the charge:

  • Restaurants: 60% of US full-service restaurants use QR code menus. The technology saves an average of $2,800 per restaurant per year in printing costs.
  • Retail packaging: 23% of consumer packaged goods now include a QR code. Most link to recycling instructions, traceability data, or loyalty programs.
  • Out-of-home advertising: 41% of new OOH campaigns include a QR code. The scan rate is 6-9% of impressions \u2014 much higher than the click-through rate of any digital ad format.

Frequently Asked Questions

Are QR codes declining in 2025?

No. The growth rate has slowed from the 2020-2022 surge, but the absolute number of scans continues to climb 15-20% year over year in the US. The post-COVID boom is normalizing, not reversing.

Is NFC replacing QR codes?

No. NFC and QR codes serve different use cases. NFC is faster for payments but requires more expensive hardware. QR codes are essentially free to generate and scan, which makes them the default for any non-payment use case. The two technologies are coexisting, not competing.

What is the biggest risk for QR code growth?

Consumer trust. As QR codes have grown, so has QR code phishing (\u201cquishing\u201d). A 2024 study found 1 in 4 Americans had scanned a QR code that took them to a suspicious website. The industry is responding with better visual feedback, browser warnings, and a push for signed QR codes, but consumer education is still the weak link.

How big will the QR code market get?

Forecasts vary, but the consensus is that the global QR code market (generation tools, dynamic QR platforms, scan analytics) will reach $25-30 billion by 2030. The largest sub-segment will be dynamic QR codes with scan analytics, not static generation tools.

Conclusion: The QR Code Is Boring, and That\u2019s a Good Thing

The most important statistic in this report isn\u2019t any of the ones above. It\u2019s this: 64% of US consumers who scan QR codes say they don\u2019t think about the technology \u2014 they just point and tap. That\u2019s the definition of a mature technology. It\u2019s invisible. It just works.

For businesses, the implication is clear: stop debating whether to use QR codes. Start optimizing where you place them, what they link to, and how you measure scans. The technology is here. The consumers are ready. The only question is what you\u2019ll do with it.
